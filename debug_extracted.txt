History
Rationalism and structuralism: symbolic approaches
‚Ä¢ Ferdinand de Saussure (Swiss linguist, philosopher of language):
parole (particular, irregular) ‚Üîlangue (shared, structured).
‚Ä¢ Structuralism: language has an internal structure with basic constituents and composition 
rules (compositionality): the meaning of a complex expression depends on the meaning 
of its constituents and its structure, e.g. green apples = green + apple + s.
‚Ä¢ Noam Chomsky: hypothesis of an abstract universal grammar and of a mechanism
within the human brain dedicated to linguistic capacity (theory partially confirmed 
by neuroscience and experimentation on humans and animals).
‚Ä¢ Still Chomsky: formal languages, generative grammars => the analysis of language 
can be automated, provided that we have a detailed formal description of the grammar.

‚Ä¢ Statistical (and neural) methods obtain linguistic knowledge inductively, from corpora.
‚Ä¢ Requirements:
‚Ä¢ large corpora (thousands, millions, or billions of sentences);
‚Ä¢ sufficient computational power;
‚Ä¢ before the Internet (2000s), Wikipedia, etc., large corpora were hard to obtain.
‚Ä¢ Corpus engineering:
‚Ä¢ preprocessing: preparing the corpus for the downstream task:
filtering, simplifications, sentence and token segmentation, etc.;
‚Ä¢ annotation: (typically manual) extension of the corpus by linguistic information,
serving as ground truth for subsequent supervised learning.

Advantages and Limitations of Statistical ML Methods
‚Ä¢ Generalisation: supervised ML components in NLP pipelines are more robust 
and generalise better to unseen data => overall pipeline performance is higher.
‚Ä¢ Cross-domain and cross-lingual transfer: applying trained models to different domains, 
different languages, different styles, etc. usually results in significant performance drop. 
Retraining is often necessary.
‚Ä¢ Human effort: rule design is replaced by human corpus annotation:
still labour-intensive, but less error-prone, less sensitive to mistakes, and less technical.
‚Ä¢ Feature engineering: the selection of optimal features is a non-trivial empirical problem.
‚Ä¢ Pipeline effect: ML-based pipelines still suffer from cumulative errors.

Tendencies:
‚Ä¢ increase in generalisation ability (solve new tasks, on new input);
‚Ä¢ reduction of expert input needed;
‚Ä¢ reduction of NLP pipeline length;

This course will:
‚Ä¢ give you an insight into the complexity of natural language

‚Ä¢ give you a panorama of NLP paradigms;
